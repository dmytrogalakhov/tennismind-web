# TennisMind Voice

## Purpose

Produce tennis writing that makes a reader feel the match before they understand it, then leaves them with one argument they can repeat — or disagree with. The voice is not neutral recap. It is a point of view, anchored in what actually happened on court.

## Governing Principle

**Lead with the moment. Carry one thesis. Show the body, not the feeling.**

The reader should be courtside in the first three lines and arguing with you by the last one. Everything in between either drops them into the scene or builds the single message. Nothing else survives.

## The Three Things Every Piece Must Do

1. **Drop the reader into a moment** — open on a specific beat in present tense, not a warm-up paragraph.
2. **Thread one thesis** — a single, debatable claim, set up early, evidenced in the middle, paid off at the close.
3. **Earn the take from the court** — the opinion is the reason the piece exists, but it is built on observed, verifiable detail, never assertion.

## The Voice, in Rules

- **Short, punchy sentences as the default.** Fragments allowed at peak tension. But the power of a short sentence comes from contrast — drop in the occasional long, flowing sentence so the short ones land like punches. If everything is staccato, nothing is.
- **Present tense for the scene.** Pull back into past/reflective tense only for context. The signature is the live, courtside present — protect it.
- **Score and number as the spine.** Recurring score callouts ("5-1", "0-15", "two sets and 5-1") are punctuation and motif. They carry the suspense; let them recur.
- **Physical tells over emotion labels.** Write what you would see, not what it means. "Sinner massages his legs / hands on his knees / staring at his box" — not "he looks lost and panicked." Trust the reader to feel it.
- **Concrete over abstract** in every sentence where there's a choice.
- **Facts first, always.** Verify the result, score, names, key stats, and quotes before writing a word. A wrong score or misspelled name destroys trust faster than any prose can rebuild it.
- **Paraphrase quotes.** If a direct quote is essential, keep it under 15 words and use one per source. Default to reporting what was said in your own words.

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Verify the facts
Search for and lock the result, the set/game scores, the decisive stats, the streak or stakes, and any quotes. Write nothing from memory.

### Step 2 — Find the one message
State, in a single sentence, the thing the reader should leave with. ("It wasn't the heat — Sinner has no Plan B when his legs go.") If you can't write it in one sentence, the thinking isn't sharp enough yet. This sentence governs every later choice.

### Step 3 — Find the moment to open on
Pick the dramatic inflection — the turn, the collapse, the break point. Write it in present tense, staccato, with score callouts. This is the cold open. No setup before it.

### Step 4 — Map the zoom
Plan the alternation: scene (zoom in) → context (zoom out) → scene (zoom in). The wide passages (the streak, the stakes, the conditions) are where the longer sentences live and where the thesis gets its setup. The contrast between the two lenses is what makes it an article and not a transcript.

### Step 5 — Thread the thesis
Walk each section and confirm it does one of three jobs: sets up the message, gives evidence for it, or pays it off. If a passage does none, cut it or repoint it. The message should appear at least three times: as a question early, as evidence in the middle, as the landing at the end.

### Step 6 — Convert labels to tells
Pass through and replace every "he looked / seemed / felt X" with the physical thing you would observe. Keep at most one or two earned abstractions.

### Step 7 — Tune the rhythm
Read it aloud. All fragments → insert one long breath. Dragging → cut. The target is varied, not uniform.

### Step 8 — Land the close on the message
End on the thesis, not a recap of the result. The last line should be the argument, distilled. ("The heat took his legs. What beat him was having nothing else.")

## Companion Formats (same voice, shorter)

The article voice compresses into TennisMind's social channels. Same hook DNA, less room.

**LinkedIn (build-in-public register).** Open with a contrarian or curiosity hook, often the "everyone says X — I think Y" shape. Short lines with white space. Emoji as section markers (🆕 🔮 ❌ 🔄 🔴🟡🟢 ✅ 💡 →). Honest about iteration and failure. When relevant, a compact "what I learned" list with emoji bullets. Always close with the links (Substack / website / Telegram). The article's thesis becomes the hook; the link is the payoff.

**Telegram (broadcast blurb).** Three beats, no more: one hook line, the thesis in a sentence, the link. A couple of emoji. Tight enough to read on a phone between metro stops.

## Self-Checks to Run on Your Own Draft

1. **"I warmed up instead of opening."** Does the first line drop the reader into a moment, or set the table?
2. **"My take is decoration, not spine."** Is the thesis threaded through every section, or mentioned once and abandoned?
3. **"I told them how to feel."** How many emotion labels survived that should have been physical tells?
4. **"It's all one gear."** Is the rhythm varied, or wall-to-wall fragments that exhaust the reader?
5. **"I wrote from memory."** Is every score, name, and stat verified against a current source?

## Quality Checks Before Sharing

- [ ] Facts verified against a current source (result, scores, names, stats, quotes)
- [ ] Single thesis statable in one sentence, threaded through every section
- [ ] Opens on a concrete moment in present tense — no warm-up
- [ ] Score / number motif recurs as a spine
- [ ] At least one zoom-out passage for contrast, with a longer sentence or two
- [ ] Emotion labels replaced with physical tells wherever possible
- [ ] Rhythm varied — short punch against the occasional long line
- [ ] Quotes paraphrased, or under 15 words if direct
- [ ] Close lands on the message, not a recap

## Common Failure Modes

- **All staccato.** A whole piece in short fragments is exhausting and flattens the peaks. Contrast is the engine.
- **Telling the feeling.** "He looked devastated" is weaker than the towel over the head. Show it.
- **Burying the take.** Recapping the match and slipping the opinion in at the end. The argument is why the piece exists — build toward it from line one.
- **The decoration thesis.** Stating the message once instead of threading it. The reader should hit it three times.
- **Writing from memory.** Guessing a score or a streak length. One wrong number costs more trust than ten good sentences earn.
- **Generic openers.** "In a stunning turn of events..." / "This was a fascinating clash." Banned. Open on the specific.

## Deliverable

A publish-ready piece in the TennisMind voice — a Substack article in Markdown, or the matching LinkedIn / Telegram variants — leading with a moment, carrying one thesis, and landing on the argument.
