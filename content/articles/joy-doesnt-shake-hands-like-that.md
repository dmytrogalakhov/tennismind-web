Serena said she came back for the joy. Her handshake said otherwise.

Centre Court. 6-3, 6-7, 6-3. Four years away, and it ends like this — the greatest of all time, beaten in the first round by a 20-year-old ranked 53rd who hadn't won a match in eleven tries.

Maya Joint had waited her whole life for this. She'd said it at media day, wide-eyed: dreamed about playing Serena since she was a kid. Up at 2 a.m. the night before, too rattled to sleep. She walks out, forgets her warm-up, legs not moving — and then she beats her idol on the biggest court in tennis.

And she gets to the net for the moment she'll tell her grandchildren about.

Watch the handshake.

Quick. Cool. Eyes already somewhere else. The barest touch of hands and Serena is gone — no arm, no word held a beat longer, none of the warmth a legend can hand a kid for free on the day that kid's life changes. Joint's biggest win. Serena's coldest few seconds.

Then the press room. The mandatory conference. Empty chair.

Here's the thing that sits wrong. Serena told everyone this comeback was about enjoying it — playing for the love, the fun, the second act on her own terms. The 44-year-old who didn't need to be here, back for joy.

But joy doesn't shake hands like that. Joy doesn't skip the room.

The question is whether she came back for the reason she said. Because none of it looked like fun. The tennis was ferocious — a saved match point, a 122-mph serve at 44. The handshake was ice. That's not a woman playing for the love of it and losing gracefully. That's someone who came to win and couldn't stand that she didn't.

She forced a third set. Saved a match point. Hit 122 miles an hour at 44. The talent walked back onto that court after four years and it was still there — nobody's arguing she can't play.

One hug. That's all it would have cost. Maya Joint would have carried it forever.

She got a cold hand and an empty chair instead.
